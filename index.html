Graduated and Shut Out

International Graduates Finding Jobs in a Tough U.S. Market

By Jiarui Liu

Oleksandra Domaskina, the HR specialist at Velox Clearing, a technology firm that offers services to brokers and advisors, had to turn down a top job candidate, a foreign student who had just earned her master’s degree. “We really liked her, really wanted to hire her, but we just couldn’t,” said Oleksandra recalling that the student only had six months left on her visa. Without the ability to sponsor her, “it’s just wasting her time and our time.” It wasn’t about her qualifications or potential. The problem was time.

This experience is increasingly common and this high-stakes race against the clock forces students from abroad to make difficult choices long before graduation. Many feel pressured to prioritize employers known for their sponsorship programs over roles that better match their skills or passions. Across the U.S., international graduates are discovering that a diploma, no matter how prestigious, doesn’t guarantee a career path. Instead, they face two significant challenges, a cooling job market and an immigration system that can block their future before it begins. The unemployment rate for recent college grads rose to 9.3% in August, nearly double what it was at the start of the year, and the pressure is even greater for international students, who must not only find a job but also find an employer willing and able to sponsor them through a complex and expensive visa process.
